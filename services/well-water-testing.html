Common Well Water Contaminants in Ontario

Professional laboratory testing identifies these critical health risks in well water

Health Risk

E. coli & Coliform Bacteria

Indicates fecal contamination from septic systems or animal waste. Causes severe gastrointestinal illness, especially dangerous for children and elderly.

Health Risk

Nitrates & Nitrites

From agricultural runoff and fertilizers. Causes methemoglobinemia (blue baby syndrome) in infants. Long-term exposure linked to cancer.

Toxic

Arsenic & Heavy Metals

Naturally occurring in Ontario bedrock. Arsenic causes cancer, skin lesions, cardiovascular disease. Requires specialized testing to detect.

Corrosive

Lead & Copper

From plumbing corrosion in older homes. Lead causes developmental delays in children and neurological damage. Copper causes liver and kidney damage.

Aesthetic

Iron, Manganese & Hardness

Stains fixtures and laundry, causes scaling in pipes and appliances. While not health risks, they damage plumbing and reduce water quality.

Chemical

Pesticides & Herbicides

Agricultural and lawn care chemicals leaching into groundwater. Long-term exposure linked to cancer, hormonal disruption, and neurological effects.

Ontario Well Water Testing Regulations

Understanding legal requirements for private well water testing in Ontario

Regulation 170/03

Ontario Drinking Water Systems Regulation

Mandates that owners of private wells must ensure water is safe to drink. While not requiring specific testing frequency, establishes maximum acceptable concentrations for contaminants.

Health Canada

Guidelines for Canadian Drinking Water Quality

Establishes maximum acceptable concentrations (MACs) for 75+ contaminants. Health Canada recommends testing private wells at least 3 times per year for bacteria and annually for chemicals.

Real Estate

Property Transfer Requirements

Most real estate transactions require recent well water testing. Buyers typically require tests less than 30 days old for bacteria and less than 6 months for chemical analysis.

Well Water Testing FAQ

Common questions about well water testing, contamination, and Ontario regulations

How often should I test my well water in Ontario?
Health Canada recommends testing private wells at least 3 times per year for bacterial contamination (spring, summer, fall) and annually for chemical contaminants. Additionally, test after any flooding, nearby construction, septic system issues, or changes in water taste/color/odor. Real estate transactions require testing within 30 days for bacteria and 6 months for chemicals.
What happens if my well tests positive for E. coli?
If E. coli is detected: 1) Immediately stop drinking the water, 2) Boil all water for at least 1 minute before use, 3) Shock chlorinate the well system, 4) Retest after 48 hours, 5) Investigate contamination source (septic system, animal waste, surface water intrusion). Continuous positive results may require well rehabilitation or replacement.
Is well water testing required for real estate transactions in Ontario?
While not legally mandated by provincial law, most lenders require well water testing before approving mortgages. Standard practice includes bacterial testing (less than 30 days old) and chemical analysis (less than 6 months old). Purchase agreements often include well water conditions requiring satisfactory test results. Professional testing provides legal protection for both buyers and sellers.
